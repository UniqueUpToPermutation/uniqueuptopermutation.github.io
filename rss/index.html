The Photon

Let's first start by thinking about the simplest object involved in light transfer: the photon. If you're interested in what a photon actually is - an excitation of a quantum field - you'll have to learn quantum field theory, but for our purposes, imagine that a photon is just a discrete particle of light travelling through space. A photon has a few elementary properties, including:

  • Position: At any given time \(t\), every photon has a position \(X_t \in \mathbb{R}^3\).

  • Direction: At any given time \(t\), every photon has a direction \(\mathbf{\omega}_t \in \mathbb{S}^2\). While direction may vary, the speed of a photon in free space is always constant as \(c \approx 3 \cdot 10^8 \, m/s\).

  • Wavelength: A photon has a specific wavelength \(\lambda > 0\). The electromagnetic field carrying a photon usually has a periodicity and \(\lambda\) is the length of a single period.

  • Frequency: A photon has a frequency \(\nu\). The frequency measures the time periodicity at which the underlying electromagnetic field - locally, the oscillations of electromagnetic field returns to its original configuration every \(1/\nu\) seconds. The frequency and wavelength are related by the speed of the photon,

    $$\lambda \nu = c \,.$$

  • Energy: A photon carries with it a certain amount of energy. This energy comes from the light source that generate the photon via spontaneous or stimulated emission. The Planck-Einstein relation postulates that the energy a photon carries is directly porpotional to its frequency. The constant of porportionality is called the Planck constant \(h\):

    $$E = h\nu\,.$$

  • Momentum: As postulated by de Broglie, the momentum \(p\) of a particle is inversely porpotional to its wavelength, i.e.,

    $$p = \frac{h}{\mu} = \frac{E}{c} \,.$$

For our purposes the important parts of a photon are really only its position, direction, and wavelength. In the coming sections, we will therefore focus on \((X_t, \omega_t, \lambda)\).

Photon Motion in Free Space

Photons don't just sit there, they tend to move around. In fact, they move around at the speed of light (which is pretty fast). Therefore, if we're describing the motion of a single photon \((X_t, \omega_t, \lambda)\) in free space, it should satisfy the basic equation of motion:

$$\frac{dX_t}{dt} = c \, \omega_t \,.$$

In computer graphics, photons tend not to interact with each other, so if we have a collection of photons \((X_t^{(i)}, \omega_t^{(i)}, \lambda^{(i)})\), the equations are motions are not coupled in any way:

$$\frac{dX_t^{(i)}}{dt} = c \, \omega_t^{(i)} \,.$$

Note that to complete the equations, we need to specify the directions \(\omega_t^{(i)}\). In free space, once a photon has been released, it will retain its direction and speed as long as it doesn't hit anything. Therefore, the free space equations for photon motion are exceedingly simple, as the directions \(\omega_t^{(i)}\) are all constants \(\omega^{(i)}\):

$$\frac{dX_t^{(i)}}{dt} = c \, \omega^{(i)} \,.$$

Starting from initial positions \(X_0^{(i)}\), these ODEs are very easy to solve:

$$X_t^{(i)} = X_0^{(i)} + c \omega^{(i)} t \,.$$

Note that this analysis is in terms of individual non-interacting particles \(X_t^{(i)}\). This is usually refered to as a Lagrangian formulation. Most of the time however, the motion of individual particles is not the end goal. Instead, we are often interested the distribution of particles \(\rho_t\). This distribution \(\rho_t(x, \omega)\) is specified as a function of both a position in space \(x \in \mathbb{R}^3\) and a direction \(\omega \in \mathbb{S}^2\). Note that the evolution of \(\rho_t(x, \omega)\) is not tied to the viewpoint of the individual particles, but rather to the viewpoint of individual locations in free space. This distribution-based view is referred to as an Eulerian formulation.

How does one go from the Lagrangian formulation to an Eulerian formulation? One way is via a technique known as characteristic analysis. Note that the equations of motion are completely reversible - if I specify the position and orientation of a photon at time \(t\), you can calculate backwards the position and orientation at time \(0\). Therefore, all particles follow non-intersecting characteristic paths \((X_t, \omega_t)\) through the phase space \(\mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{S}^2\). This means that the distribution of particles along these paths should remain constant over time. This gives the equation:

$$\frac{d\rho_t(X_t, \omega_t)}{dt} = 0 \,.$$

Using the chain rule,

$$\frac{d\rho_t(X_t, \omega_t)}{dt} = \nabla \rho_t(X_t, \omega_t) \cdot \frac{dX_t}{dt} + \frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial \omega_t}(X_t, \omega_t) \cdot \frac{d\omega_t}{dt} + \frac{\partial \rho_t(X_t, \omega_t)}{\partial t} = 0 \,.$$

In the particular case of free space, \(\omega_t\) is constant, therefore, using the equation for \(dX_t / dt\) from above, we get:

$$c \omega \cdot \nabla \rho_t(x, \omega) + \frac{\partial \rho_t(x, \omega)}{\partial t} = 0\,.$$

Rearranged:

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t(x, \omega)}{\partial t} = c \omega \cdot \nabla \rho_t(x, \omega)\,.$$

If we fix \(\omega\), you might recognize this as the transport equation from partial differential equations.

Scattering in Homogenous Media

Of course, the above equations are very simplistic. They simply say that photons move in straight lines when in free space. Though this is useful, often-times photons are moving through a medium where they constantly collide with other particles and are scattered and absorbed throughout. The different ways materials scatter light are wholy reasonable for the different ways they look. For example, in the above figure, light enters the lion and then scatters around inside it until it exists and hits the viewer's eye. This gives the impression of soft lighting and partial translucency (see how the tail lets some light through with its red-orange highlights). So, if we are going to talk about light transport with an eye towards rendering things like the statue above, we are going to need to discuss how light changes direction.

Unfortunately, this process is not deterministic. Or at least, it is not deterministic from the macroscopic viewpoint. Two light particles can hit the same surface and be reflected in completely different directions. We model this by saying that a light particle hitting a material (or fluid) at location \(x\) in direction \(\omega_i\) has a certain probability of being redirected in direction \(\omega_o\). It also has a certain probability of being absorbed. In the most general case, we want to think about rates rather than concrete probabilities. When a photon enters a scattering medium like the one pictured above, the photon will move a certain distance until it impacts something in the medium that redirects it. You should think of the contents of the medium as being random, and hence the distance travelled before a photon impacts an obstacle will be random as well. The average distance travelled before a photon hits an obstacle (i.e. scatters) is called the mean free path. The higher the mean free path, the more the photon can travel through the medium unobstructed and therefore the clearer the medium.

Let us take this information and think about the parameters of a particle \((X_t, \omega_t)\) travels through a homogenous scattering medium. Occasionally \(\omega_t\) will suddenly change randomly when the particle hits something. But how do we characterize this randomness?

We can model this scattering process by using a continuous \(Q\)-process on the sphere \(\mathbb{S}^2\). Recall that a \(Q\)-process \(W_t\) on \(\mathbb{S}^2\) is a continous time random process whose value changes abruptly at random times \(T_1, T_2, ..., T_n, ...\). In particular, a \(Q\)-process on \(\mathbb{S}^2\) is characterized by a generator operator \(\mathcal{L}\) which satisfies

$$\mathcal{L}(\omega_i, \omega_o) = \sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o) - \mu_s(\omega_i) \, \delta(\omega_i, \omega_o) \,,$$

where

$$\mu_s(\omega) = \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \sigma(\omega, \omega_o) \, d\Omega(\omega_o) \,.$$

We'll talk more about \(\mathcal{L}\) later, but for now, let's focus on \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o)\). You should think of the function \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o)\) as the probability transition rates to go from a state \(x \rightarrow y\). That is, \(Y_t\) satisfies:
$$\mathbb{P}(W_{t + dt} \in d\Omega (\omega_o) \mid W_t = \omega_i) = \sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o) \, d\Omega \, dt + o(dt) \,.$$
In words, after a very small amount of time \(dt\), \(W_t\) has a probability of about \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o) \, d\Omega \, dt\) to jump to a small solid angle \(d\Omega\) around \(\omega_o\). This function \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o)\) is called the phase function and determines the rate at which a photon travelling in direction \(\omega_i\) is redirected to direction \(\omega_o\) after it hits an obstacle.

How do we go from a Lagrangian formulation of a \(Q\)-process to an Eulerian formulation? Well let us ignore the position of our particles (the medium is homogenous, so position doesn't really matter when it comes to rotation) and simply examine the probability distribution of the directions \(\omega^{(i)}\) of our particles. We write the distribution of directions of our particles as \(\rho_t(\omega)\). You should think of this function as a representing the fact that there are \(\rho_t(\omega)\) photons anywhere in the medium that are in direction \(\omega\).

Let us compute how the density \(\rho_t(\omega)\) changes over time at a given orientation \(\omega\). From the definition of the \(Q\)-process above, we have that

$$\rho_{t + dt}(\omega) = \rho_t(\omega) + \left[\int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(\omega_i)\, \sigma(\omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i) \right] dt$$

$$ - \left[ \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(\omega) \, \sigma(\omega, \omega_o) \, d\Omega(\omega_o)\right] dt + o(dt)\,.$$

This expression is saying that the number of particles in direction \(\omega\) at time \(t + dt\) is the number of particles in direction \(\omega\) at time \(t\), plus the number of particles that are redirected from any other direction \(\omega_i\) into direction \(\omega\) over the time \(dt\), minus the number of particles that are redirected from direction \(\omega\) to any other direction \(\omega_o\) over the time \(dt\). Note that we can take \(\rho_t(\omega)\) out of the integral above to get

$$\rho_{t + dt}(\omega) = \rho_t(\omega) + \left[\int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(\omega_i)\, \sigma(\omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i) \right] dt - \mu_s(\omega) \, \rho_t(\omega) \, dt + o(dt) \,.$$

Rearranging the above and taking \(dt \rightarrow 0\) gives us

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t} (\omega) = \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(\omega_i) \sigma(\omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i) - \mu_s(\omega)\, \rho_t(\omega) \,.$$

Note that the right hand side acts on the function \(\rho_t\) by applying the generator \(\mathcal{L}\):

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t} (\omega) = \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(\omega_i) \, \mathcal{L}(\omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i)\,.$$

This often appreviated by writing

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t} = \mathcal{L}^\ast \rho_t \,,$$

where \(\mathcal{L}^\ast\) is the adjoint of \(\mathcal{L}\) (i.e., \(\mathcal{L}^\ast(\omega_i, \omega_o) = \mathcal{L}(\omega_o, \omega_i)\)). This equation is called the forward equation for a \(Q\)-process. Here, one says that the operator \(\mathcal{L}^\ast\) generates the time evolution of \(\rho_t\). This is very similar to deriving the forward equations for a random walk by using Ito's lemma.

The Statistics of Scattering Events

The next question one might ask is for the actual statistics of the scattering times \(T_1\), \(T_2\), \(...\), \(T_n\), \(...\). These are relatively straightforward. Note that the \(Q\)-process is Markovian and hence the intervals \(\Delta T_i = T_i - T_{i - 1}\) are independent and identically distributed. This makes sense, because once the particle has scattered, it doesn't care about anything other than its current position and orientation. In other words, the path a particle has taken to get to a certain point is irrelevant, it is only the current state that matters. To derive statistics for the \(\Delta T_i\), we need to consider the distribution \(\rho^{(0)}_t(\omega)\). \(\rho^{(0)}_t(\omega)\) is defined as the distribution of particles that have not yet scattered. At any given time the probability that a particle scatters in the next \(dt\) seconds is given by:

$$\mathbb{P}(\text{Particle moving in direction } \omega \text{ scatters in next } dt \text{ seconds})$$

$$= \left[\int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \sigma(\omega, \omega_o) \, d\Omega(\omega_o) \right] dt + o(dt) = \mu_s(\omega) \, dt + o(dt) \,.$$

Let \(N[t, s]\) be the number of scattering events between times \(t\) and \(s\). We can determine the distribution of the scattering time \(T\) as follows (we simply write \(T\) here for a random variable with the distribution of \(\Delta T_i\)):

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t)) = \mathbb{P}(N[0, t] = 0 \text{ and } N[t, t + dt] = 1)$$

For disjoint intervals \(A\) and \(B\), a property of the \(Q\)-process is that \(N(A)\) and \(N(B)\) are independent. Therefore, we have that

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t))$$

$$= \mathbb{P}(N[0, t] = 0) \mathbb{P}(N[t, t + dt] = 1)$$

$$= \mathbb{P}(N[t, t + dt] = 1) \prod_{n = 0}^{t / dt - 1} \mathbb{P}(N[n \, dt, (n + 1) \, dt] = 0) \,,$$

We now use the fact that \(\mathbb{P}(N[t, t + dt] = 1) = \mu_s(\omega) \, dt + o(dt)\) and \(\mathbb{P}(N[t, t + dt] = 0) = 1 - \mu_s(\omega) \, dt + o(dt)\) to get:

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t)) = (\mu_s(\omega) \, dt) \left(1 - \mu_s(\omega)dt \right)^{t / dt} \,.$$

Recall that

$$\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = e \,.$$

Therefore, we get

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t)) = \mu_s(\omega) e^{-\mu_s(\omega) t} \,.$$

This is the exponential distribution with rate \(\lambda = \mu_s(\omega)\), hence the times between scattering events have distribution

$$T \sim \text{Exp}(\mu_s(\omega)) \,.$$

A nice outcome of this fact is that we can easily compute the mean scattering time to be:

$$\mathbb{E}[T] = 1 / \mu_s(\omega) \,.$$

The mean free path is therefore

$$\ell = \mathbb{E}[cT] = c / \mu_s(\omega) \,.$$

If the medium is isotropic (i.e., the function \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o)\) is rotationally invariant), then \(\mu_s(\omega)\) does not depend on \(\omega\) and this can just be written as

$$\ell = c / \mu_s \,.$$

Full Eulerian Formulation

One can obtain a full Eulerian formulation for the evolution of \(\rho_t(x, \omega)\) as follows. Let us consider the number of particles in a set \(A\) with direction \(\omega\), i.e.,

$$\int_A \rho_t(x, \omega) \, dx \,.$$

The change in the number of particles in \(A\) moving in direction \(\omega\) is given by the particle flux through the boundary \(\partial A\) as well as the particles inside \(A\) that scatter either from some direction \(\omega_i\) to \(\omega\) or scatter from direction \(\omega\) to some direction \(\omega_o\). We can write this as

$$\int_A \rho_{t + dt} (x, \omega) \, dx = \int_A \rho_{t} (x, \omega) \, dx $$

$$- \, dt \int_{\partial A} \rho_t(x, \omega) (c \,\omega \cdot dn) + dt \int_A \mathcal{L}_\omega^\ast \rho_t(x, \omega) \, dx\,.$$

The \(c \, dt \int_{\partial A} \rho_t(x, \omega) \cdot dn\) represents the sum of all particles that move out of the region \(A\) over a time interval \(dt\) and the \(dt \int_A \mathcal{L}_\omega^\ast \rho_t(x, \omega) \, dx\) represents the change in particle number from scattering inside the region \(A\) (as we derived two sections ago). Stokes' Theorem can be applied to the term over \(\partial A\) to get

$$\int_A \rho_{t + dt} (x, \omega) \, dx = \int_A \rho_{t} (x, \omega) \, dx$$

$$- c \, dt \int_{A} \omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho_t(x, \omega) \, dx + dt \int_A \mathcal{L}_\omega^\ast \rho_t(x, \omega) \, dx\,.$$

Since this is true for any \(A\), it must be the case that

$$\rho_{t + dt} (x, \omega) = \rho_{t} (x, \omega) - c \, \omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho_t(x, \omega) \, dt + \mathcal{L}_\omega^\ast \rho_t(x, \omega) \, dt \, .$$

Now rearranging and letting \(dt \rightarrow 0\),

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t}(x, \omega) = - c \, \omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho_t(x, \omega) + \mathcal{L}_\omega^\ast \rho_t(x, \omega) \, .$$

Voila. This is the Eulerian formulation of light transport in a homogenous medium with phase function \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o)\). Writing it out in full gives:

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t}(x, \omega) = - c \, \omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho_t(x, \omega) + \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(x, \omega') \sigma(\omega', \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega') - \mu_s(\omega) \rho_t(x, \omega).$$

For comparison, the Langrangian form of these equations is
$$\frac{dX_t}{dt} = c \, W_t \,,$$

where \(W_t\) is a \(Q\)-process on \(\mathbb{S}^2\) with generator \(\mathcal{L}\).

Isotropic Scattering

The simplest example of a scattering medium is one that is fully isotropic, i.e., regardless of the incomming and outgoing directions \(\omega_i\) and \(\omega_o\), the rate of scattering is the same. That is,

$$\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o) = \sigma \,.$$

Usually this is referred to as isotropic scattering. In this case, the scattering coefficient \(\mu_s\) does not depend on \(\omega\) and is easily calculated to be

$$\mu_s = 4 \pi \sigma\,.$$

The equations in the previous section now take on the form

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t}(x, \omega) = - c \, \omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho_t(x, \omega) + \mu_s \bar{\rho_t}(x) - \mu_s \rho_t(x, \omega).$$

Here \(\bar{\rho}_t\) is shorthand for the average photon density at \(x\) over all directions \(\omega\),

$$\bar{\rho}_t(x) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(x, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega) \,.$$

Scattering in Heterogenous and Absorbing Media

In the previous examples, we assumed that the scattering medium was the same everywhere, i.e., that the phase function \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o)\) did not depend on \(x\). In this section, we see what happens when the phase function \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o)\) does depend on \(x\). This is relatively common, many objects are not entirely uniform. For example, your hand scatters light, but your hand is not uniformly made of the same material. There's skin, muscle, bone, blood, etc. And each of these materials have different properties. How do we model this? Well, now the statistics of the direction \(\omega_t\) of a particle will depend heavily on that particle's position and path.

If this is the case, then \(W_t\) is now a \(Q\)-process whose phase function \(\sigma(\omega_i, \omega_o)\) changes depending on the position \(X_t\) of the particle. We will write the new phase function as \(\sigma(x; \omega_i, \omega_o)\). This means that the generator of the process now depends explicitly on the value of \(x\). Hence, we have

$$\mathcal{L}(x, \omega_i, \omega_o) = \sigma(x, \omega_i, \omega_o) - \mu_s(x, \omega_i) \, \delta(\omega_i, \omega_o) \,,$$

where once again,

$$\mu_s(x, \omega) = \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \sigma(x, \omega, \omega_o) \, d\Omega(\omega_o) \,.$$

In addition to this, we also give a particle a certain probability of being absorbed at any point in time. This probability is quantified by the absorption coefficient \(\mu_a(x, \omega)\) which is defined in a similar way to the phase function:

$$\mathbb{P}(\text{Particle with direction } \omega \text{ at } x \text{ is absorbed over a period } dt)$$

$$= \mu_a(x, \omega) \, dt + o(dt)$$

What this means is that the \(Q\)-process \(W_t\) now has a chance of jump to a dead state from which it can never return. Therefore, the number of particles in a region \(A\) now changes over a small period \(dt\) according to:

$$\int_A \rho_{t + dt}(x, \omega) \, dx = \int_A \rho_t(x, \omega) \,dx - \left[\int_{\partial A} \rho_t(x, \omega) (c\,\omega \cdot dn) \right] dt$$

$$+ \, \left[\int_A \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(x, \omega_i)\, \sigma(x, \omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i) \, dx \right] dt$$

$$ - \, \left[ \int_A \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(x, \omega) \, \sigma(x, \omega, \omega_o) \, d\Omega(\omega_o) \, dx\right] dt $$

$$- \left[\int_A \mu_a(x, \omega) \, \rho_t(x, \omega) \, dx\right]\, dt+ o(dt)\,.$$

The terms with a \(dt\) factor represent, in order of appearence:

  1. The net number of particles that leave the region \(A\).
  2. The number of particles that are scattered in \(A\) from some direction \(\omega_i\) to \(\omega\)
  3. The number of particles that are scattered in \(A\) from \(\omega\) to some other direction \(\omega_o\).
  4. The number of particles absorbed in \(A\).

Repeating the procedure of the previous section will give us:

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t} (x, \omega) = - c \,\omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho_t(x, \omega) + \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(x, \omega_i)\, \sigma(x, \omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i)$$

$$- \, (\mu_s(x, \omega) + \mu_a(x, \omega)) \rho_t(x, \omega) \,.$$

Alternatively, we can just write this as:

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t} = \mathcal{L}_{x, \omega}^\ast \rho_t \,,$$

Where the generator \(\mathcal{L}_{x, \omega}\) of the full joint process \((X_t, W_t)\) is given by:

$$\mathcal{L}_{x, \omega} \rho(x, \omega) = c \, \omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho_t(x, \omega) + \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \, \sigma(x, \omega, \omega_o) \rho_t(x, \omega_o)\, d\Omega(w_o)$$
$$ - \, (\mu_s(x, \omega) + \mu_a(x, \omega)) \rho_t(x, \omega)$$

We have therefore derived the full radiative transport equation.

Statistics of Scattering Events

Let us assume for now that the scattering medium is non-absorbing, i.e., that \(\mu_a = 0\) everywhere. Just like in the homogenous case, every particle will have a series of scattering times \(T_1, T_2, ..., T_n, ...\). The difference now is that these scattering times depend explicitly on the path the photon is travelling along.

We can calculate the statistics of scattering events by using the same trick as last time. Let \(N[t, s]\) be the number of scattering events in the time interval \([t, s]\). Suppose that the particle has direction \(\omega\) and is traveling along the ray \(X_t^{(0)} = X_0 + c \omega t\). The we have that:

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t)) = \mathbb{P}(N[t, t + dt] = 1) \prod_{n = 0}^{t / dt - 1} \mathbb{P}(N[n \,dt, (n + 1) \,dt] = 0)$$

$$ = \mu_s(X_t^{(0)}, \omega) \prod_{n = 0}^{t/dt - 1}\left(1 - \mu_s(X_{n\, dt}) dt \right)$$

When \(x \ll 1\), we know that \(1 - x \sim \exp(-x)\). Therefore,

$$1 - \mu_s(X_{n \, dt})dt \sim \exp(-\mu_s(X_{n \, dt}) \, dt ) \,.$$

We then have:

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t)) = \mu_s(X_t^{(0)}, \omega) \exp \left( -\sum_{n = 0}^{t / dt - 1} \mu_s(X_{n \, dt}^{(0)}, \omega) \, dt\right) \, dt\,. $$

As \(dt \rightarrow 0\) this converges to

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t)) = \mu_s(X_t^{(0)}, \omega) \exp \left( -\int_0^t \mu_s(X_{s}^{(0)}, \omega) \, ds\right) \, dt\,.$$

Therefore, if one wishes to simulate a photon scattering in a heterogenous medium, one might sample scattering times from the above distribution.

Statistics of Absorption Events

The above section only covers scattering events. However, a photon may experience either a scattering or an absorption event while it is traversing through a medium. Recall, that photons travelling in direction \(\omega\) at a position \(x\) are absorbed at a rate \(\mu_a(\omega)\). Combining this with scattering events, at every time step of \(dt\), a photon has a probability \(\mu_a(\omega) \, dt\) of being absorbed and a \(\mu_s(\omega) \, dt\) probability of being scattered. Let \(A\) be the event where the photon is absorbed. Therefore, one can follow the derivation of the previous section to obtain:

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t), A) = \mu_a(X_t^{(0)}, \omega) \exp\left(- \int_0^t \left(\mu_s(X_s^{(0)}, \omega) + \mu_a(X_s^{(0)}, \omega) \right) \, ds\right)\, dt$$

$$\mathbb{P}(T \in dt(t), \bar{A}) = \mu_s(X_t^{(0)}, \omega) \exp\left(- \int_0^t \left(\mu_s(X_s^{(0)}, \omega) + \mu_a(X_s^{(0)}, \omega) \right) \, ds\right)\, dt$$

Therefore, the probability that the photon is absorbed is given by:

$$\mathbb{P}(A) = \int_0^\infty \mu_a(X_t^{(0)}, \omega) \exp\left(- \int_0^t \left(\mu_s(X_s^{(0)}, \omega) + \mu_a(X_s^{(0)}, \omega) \right) \, ds\right)\, dt$$

Similarly, the probability that the photon is scattered is given by:

$$\mathbb{P}(\bar{A}) = \int_0^\infty \mu_s(X_t^{(0)}, \omega) \exp\left(- \int_0^t \left(\mu_s(X_s^{(0)}, \omega) + \mu_a(X_s^{(0)}, \omega) \right) \, ds\right)\, dt$$

If the media is homogenous, that is if \(\mu_s(x, \omega) = \mu_s(\omega)\) and \(\mu_a(x, \omega) = \mu_a(\omega)\), this reduces to:

$$\mathbb{P}(A) = \frac{\mu_a(\omega)}{\mu_a(\omega) + \mu_s(\omega)} \,.$$

$$\mathbb{P}(\bar{A}) = \frac{\mu_s(\omega)}{\mu_a(\omega) + \mu_s(\omega)} \,.$$

Emissive Media

In addition to absorbing and scattering light, a general medium can also emit photons. To quantify this emission, we introduce an emission rate \(j(x, \omega)\). The quantity of light emitted at \(x\) in direction \(\omega\) over a short period \(dt\) is given by

$$j(x, \omega, t) \, dt \,.$$

Therefore, it is relatively simple to see that we should update the Eulerian equations by adding a \(j(x, \omega)\) term to the right hand side:

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t}(x, \omega) = \mathcal{L}_{x, \omega}^\ast \rho_t (x, \omega) + j(x, \omega, t)\,.$$

Or written out in full:

$$\frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t} (x, \omega) = - c \,\omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho_t(x, \omega) + \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(x, \omega_i)\, \sigma(x, \omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i)$$

$$- \, (\mu_s(x, \omega) + \mu_a(x, \omega)) \rho_t(x, \omega) + j(x, \omega, t)\,.$$

Lights are examples of emissive materials.

Steady-State Radiative Transfer

Often times, we aren't interested in the evolution of the distribution \(\rho_t(x, \omega)\) over a short time scale, but rather, we're interested in the average distribution over a very long time. Our eyes act like integrators, so if we want to talk about rendering, we care about the time averaged distribution of photons,

$$\rho(x, \omega) = \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T \rho_t(x, \omega) \,.$$

This time-averaged distribution should represent a sort of "steady state" of the system when a given \(j(x, \omega, t)\) is applied as an input. Computing this also requires time integrating out fluctuations in the emissive term \(j(x, \omega, t)\),

$$\bar{j}(x, \omega) = \lim_{T \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T j(x, \omega, t) \,.$$

At equilibrium, we expect that the distribution of light no longer changes, so an easy way to compute it is to set

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0\,.$$

This can be written out as:

$$c \,\omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho(x, \omega) + \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho(x, \omega_i)\, \sigma(x, \omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i)$$

$$+ (\mu_s(x, \omega) + \mu_a(x, \omega)) \rho(x, \omega) = \bar{j}(x, \omega) \,.$$

These are the elliptic equations for the steady state of a radiative transfer system. Note that the equations are linear in \(\rho\). Therefore, we know that the function \(\rho\) must be linear in \(\bar{j}\). This linearity can be written in terms of a scattering operator \(\mathcal{S}\) that acts on the function \(\bar{j}\). This function corresponds to applying the Green's function \(G\) of the elliptic equation above:

$$\rho(x, \omega) = \mathcal{S} \bar{j}(x, \omega) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^3} \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} G(x, \omega, x', \omega') \, \bar{j}(x', \omega') \, d\Omega(\omega') \, dx' \,.$$

Steady-State Transfer with Boundary Conditions

Often times we're not interested in scattering on the whole of \(\mathbb{R}^3\), but rather on some restricted domain \(D\). We can easily imagine the following question: given the geometry and makeup of someone's face, suppose we put a flashlight under it and want to know what the face will look like when after the light propagates through it.

From such a problem to be well posed, we need to specify an ingoing light at the boundary. We will denote this as \(f(x, \omega)\) (for "flashlight"). \(f(x, \omega)\) is defined on the boundary \(x \in \partial D\) for ingoing directions \(\omega \cdot n < 0\). Furthermore, we may have emissive materials inside the domain \(D\), which we will have to model with the function \(\bar{j}(x, \omega)\) defined on the interior of \(D\). Therefore, we have

$$c \,\omega \cdot \nabla_x \rho(x, \omega) + \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho(x, \omega_i)\, \sigma(x, \omega_i, \omega) \, d\Omega(\omega_i)$$

$$+ \, (\mu_s(x, \omega) + \mu_a(x, \omega)) \rho(x, \omega) = \bar{j}(x, \omega), \qquad \text{for } x \in \text{int}(D)\,.$$

And we have boundary conditions

$$\rho(x, \omega) = f(x, \omega), \qquad \text{for } x \in \partial D \text{ and } \omega \cdot n(x) < 0 \,.$$

In the same way as before, the output \(\rho\) is linear in the inputs \(f\) and \(\bar{j}\). It can therefore be written as:

$$\rho = \mathcal{S}_{\partial D} f + \mathcal{S}_{\text{int}(D)} \bar{j}\,,$$

where \(\mathcal{S}_{\partial D}\) and \(\mathcal{S}_{\text{int}(D)}\) are scattering operators (from boundary \(\partial D\) to \(D\) and interior \(\text{int}(D)\) to \(D\) respectively). They also have corresponding Green's functions:

$$\rho(x, \omega) = \int_{\partial D} \int_{\omega' \cdot n(x) < 0} G_{\partial D} (x, \omega, x', \omega') \, d\Omega(\omega') \, dx' $$
$$+ \int_{\partial D} \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} G_{\text{int}(D)}(x, \omega') \, d\Omega(\omega') \, dx' \,.$$

The Green's function \(G_{\partial D}(x, \omega, x', \omega')\) when the output is restricted to the boundary \(x \in \partial D\) and \(\omega \cdot n(x) > 0\) is called the bidirectional scattering distribution function (BSDF) of the object \(D\).

Radiant Flux, Irradiance, and Radiance

There are some fundamental quantities that often get tossed around in computer graphics. Before moving on to other stuff, it is good to put everything together with standard temonology. Let us go through all the necessary terms one-by-one:

  1. Radiant Flux (across a surface \(S\)): The radiant flux through \(S\) is given by the energy that moves through \(S\) per unit time. Recall that the energy per particle is given by

    $$E = hc / \lambda$$

    Therefore, for monochromatic light with wavelength \(\lambda\), the radiance flux across \(S\) is given by:

    $$\Phi_t = \frac{dE}{dt} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \int_{S} \rho_t(x, \omega) (c \omega \cdot dn) \, d\Omega(\omega) $$

  2. Irradiance: The irradiance at a location \(x\) on a surface \(S\) is a measure of the energy recieved/emitted by the surface per unit time per unit area. It is written as:

    $$I_t(x) = \frac{d\Phi}{dA} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \int_{\mathbb{S}^2} \rho_t(x, \omega) (c \omega \cdot n(x)) \, d\Omega(\omega) $$

  3. Radiance: The radiance at a location \(x\) and in direction \(\omega\) on a surface is a measure of the energy recieved/emitted by the surface per unit time per unit area per solid angle. It is often written:

    $$L_t(x, \omega) = \frac{d^2\Phi}{dA \, d\Omega} = \frac{dI}{d\Omega} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \rho_t(x, \omega) (c \omega \cdot n(x)) $$

    It is common to write \(\omega \cdot n(x)\) as \(\cos\theta\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between the direction of the photon \(\omega\) and the normal \(n(x)\) of the surface,

    $$L_t(x, \omega) = \frac{hc^2}{\lambda} \rho_t(x, \omega) \cos \theta \,.$$

Many high-fidelity physics models require a huge amount of computing resources - think simulating the drag force across an airplane. Practically, this means that important tasks like uncertainty quantification and Bayesian inference are computationally intractable, since they require not just one, but many solves of the underlying high-fidelity model. Reduced order modelling techniques use data to build fast approximate models as substitutes the original high fidelity physics model. These approximate models are useful for the aforementioned situations where one needs to query the high fidelity physics model many times in succession for roughly similar parameters.

However, much of the research in model order reduction revolves around differential equations rather than integral equations. While differential equations are more widely used in general, there are many fields and situations in which integral equations may be preferable. This is for a number of reasons:

  • Integral equations tend to be better conditioned.
  • In fields involving wave propagation (electromagnetism, acoustics, etc.) one can often convert differential equations over the interior of a region into integral equations over the boundary --- essentially reducing a sparse 3D problem into a dense 2D one.
  • Analytic approximation techniques like fast multiple method can make evaluating integral operators very efficient; making them very suitable for iterative methods.

Therefore, given the utility of integral equations and the potential speed-up model order reduction offers for tasks like uncertainty quantification and Bayesian inference, and the fact that integral equations have been under-served by the model order reduction community at large, building model order reduction techniques for integral equations is a worthwhile task. In particular, in this paper, we focus on the classical reduced basis techniques.

The classical reduced basis method roughly boils down to the following scheme:

  1. Suppose one has a (discrete PDE) linear algebra problem parameterized by a set of parameters \(\mu\) (i.e., the shape of your airplane's wings, the scattering background of your problem, etc.). We write the mathematical problem as:

$$ \mathbf{A}(\mu) \mathbf{x}(\mu) = \mathbf{b}(\mu) $$

  1. (Offline) One isolates a collection of parameters \(\mu_1, ..., \mu_n\) and solve their corresponding problems to obtain \(\mathbf{x}(\mu_1), ..., \mathbf{x}(\mu_n)\). Note this is an expensive step but can be done offline

  2. (Offline) One uses \(\mathbf{x}(\mu_1), ..., \mathbf{x}(\mu_n)\) to compute a reduced basis matrix \(\mathbf{\Phi}\).

  3. (Online) For a new \(\mu'\), we can solve the much lower dimensional Galerkin projection of the original problem:

$$ (\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{A}(\mu') \mathbf{\Phi}) \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{b}(\mu')$$

And then approximate:

$$ \mathbf{x}(\mu') \approx \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{y} $$

There is a critical issue with mixing reduced basis methods and integral equations however. Namely, most reduced basis techniques have been specially developed for the type of sparse operators that typically appear in differential equations problems. The operators in the integral equations world are extremely dense in comparison and forming the reduced operator \( \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{A}(\mu') \mathbf{\Phi} \) is difficult to do outright or using existing reduced basis techniques (discrete empirical interpolation, etc.).

There is also another issue: How to efficiently choose the samples \( \mu_1, ..., \mu_n \)? Our new method addresses both of these issues by introducing the idea of coarse proxy models.

A coarse proxy model for a physics model is essentially an a priori sloppy estimate of the output we care about. For example, instead of computing a solution on a 1024 x 1024 grid, we can compute a solution on a 64 x 64. The important part is that the coarse solution gives some impression as to the "important" parameter instances. By using a column pivoted QR factorization of these sloppy coarse outputs, we can identify the "important" parameters \( \mu_1, ..., \mu_n \) in the sample space and build a reduced basis model by solving the full physics model for each of these parameters.

Furthermore, by explicitly assembling important samples of the dense corresponding operators \( \mathbf{A}(\mu_1), ..., \mathbf{A}(\mu_n) \) (which cannot be assembled in full). We can compute interpolation weights that we allow us to form reduced operators for new parameters \( \mu' \) by interpolating explicitly computed reduced operators \( \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{A}(\mu_1) \mathbf{\Phi}\), \(...\), \(\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{A}(\mu_n) \mathbf{\Phi} \) by utilizing samples from of the dense operator \( \mathbf{A}(\mu') \). This allows us to efficiently approximate new reduced operators.

We demonstrate in our paper that this technique has the ability to substantially reduce computation time when solving a large collection of parameterized integral equations. Our examples include problems in radiative transport as well as integral formulations of the Laplace equation. We can achieve significant speedups without much sacrifice to accuracy.

Moving to Continuous Stochastic Processes: Ito's Formula

Here is where we finally make the jump to continuous time and space. To begin this conversion process, we need to make note that the fundamental analogue of the stochastic graph process \(X_t\) is going to be a continuous space/time Ito process \(X_t\) defined by the stochastic differential equation

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t) \, dt + \sigma(X_t) \, dB_t,$$

where \(B_t\) is a standard Brownian motion (i.e., continuous time random walk). The term \(\mu(x)\) is referred to as the drift of the process \(X_t\) when it is at \(x\) and \(\sigma(x)\) is the volitility of the process \(X_t\) when it is at \(x\). If you've never seen stochastic differential equations before, the above equation shouldn't scare you -- you should try to read it defining as the continuous time limit of the following discrete-time process:

$$X_{t + \Delta t} - X_t = \mu(X_t) \, \Delta t + \sigma(X_t) \, \Delta B_t,$$

where \(\Delta B_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Delta t)\) are independent normal variables with mean \(0\) and variance \(\Delta t\), and the time step \(\Delta t\) is taken to be infinitesimally small.

Here is where we are going to need a bit of Ito calculus. Don't worry! It's just like normal calculus, except instead of just having a single class of standard infinitesimal quantity \(dt\), we also have a second class of "special" infinitesimal normal random variables \(dB_t\). Just like in normal elementary calculus, the way we perform any calculation is to expand in powers of \(dt\) and \(dB_t\) and then discard anything that is smaller than \(dt\) (since \(dt\) is already infinitesimally small). The caveat is that

$$dB_t \approx \sqrt{dt},$$

since \(dB_t\) is an infinitesimal normal random variable with variance \(dt\) and hence standard deviation \(\sqrt{dt}\). The way that Ito calculus expresses this equivalence is with the identity

$$(dB_t)^2 = dt.$$

Think of this like an infinitesimal version of the law of large numbers of Gaussian random variables. As \(dt\) grows infinitesimally small, the randomness in \((dB_t)^2\) basically vanishes and it becomes a deterministic quantity (in reality the randomness is still there, but it is negligible because it has order less than \(dt\), and hence \((dB_t)^2\) concentrates about its expectation, \(dt\)). Therefore, when taking a Taylor expansion with an infinitesimal variable \(dt\) and the random variable \(dB_t\) we can discard everything above first order in \(dt\) or second order in \(dB_t\).

Suppose now that \(u(x)\) is a function and we want to say something about the stochastic process \(u(X_t)\). Performing a Taylor expansion on \(u\) allows us to express the infinitesimal change in \(u(X_t)\) in terms of quantities we have already defined and know how to handle,

$$du(X_t) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} dX_t + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} (dX_t)^2$$

We ignore everything higher than second order because anything smaller than \(dt\) is effectively zero. Note that, by definition,

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t) \, dt + \sigma(X_t) \, dB_t,$$

And also,

$$(dX_t)^2 = \mu(X_t) \, dt^2 + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2(X_t) \mu(X_t) \, dB_t \, dt + \sigma(X_t)^2 \, (dB_t)^2 = \sigma(X_t)^2 , dt,$$

where we have used the identity \((dB_t)^2 = dt\), and discarded any terms that are higher order in \(dt\). Therefore,

$$du(X_t) = \mu(X_t) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \, dt + \sigma(X_t) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \, dB_t + \frac{1}{2} \sigma(X_t)^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \, dt, $$

This formula is the analogue of the chain rule for elementary calculus, and goes by the name of Ito's formula. Note that the presence of randomness adds the \(\sigma(X_t) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \, dB_t\) and \(\frac{1}{2} \sigma(X_t)^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \, dt\) terms to the otherwise standard expressions. The Ito formula is often written as follows

$$du(X_t) = \mathcal{L} u(X_t) \, dt + \sigma(X_t) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \, dB_t,$$

where \(\mathcal{L}\) is known as the infinitesimal generator of the process \(X_t\), and is given by

$$\mathcal{L} = \mu(x) \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2(x) \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}.$$

Then, from Ito's formula above, we have that

$$du(X_t) = \mathcal{L} u(X_t) \, dt + \sigma(X_t) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \, dB_t.$$

In particular, the wonderful thing about this choice of \(u\) is that, in expectation the \(dB_t\) term vanishes, since \(dB_t\) is mean zero and independent of everything that happens up until time \(t\). Thus,

$$ \mathbb{E}[du(X_t) \mid X_0 = x] = \mathbb{E}[\mathcal{L} u (X_t) \, dt \mid X_0 = x] + \mathbb{E}\left[\sigma(X_t) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \, dB_t \mid X_0 = x\right]$$

$$= \mathbb{E}[\mathcal{L} u (X_t) \, dt \mid X_0 = x]. $$

Integrating both sides from \(t = 0\) to \(t = \tau\) gives us the following formula

$$\mathbb{E}[u(X_\tau) \mid X_0 = x] - u(x) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \int_0^\tau \mathcal{L} u (X_t) \, dt \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

Or alternatively,

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \int_0^\tau -\mathcal{L} u (X_t) \, dt + u(X_\tau) \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

It is actually possible to take \(\tau\) to be a random variable (we won't show it here because that requires some analysis stuff that's beyond the scope of this tutorial), so we can replace the \(\tau\) with a stopping time \(T\). \(T\) can be (and is very often taken to be) the hitting time of some set in \(\mathbb{R}\). Often times, we let \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}\) be an interval and then define the stopping time \(T\) as the first time at which \(X_t\) hits the boundary of the domain \(\Omega\), i.e.,

$$T = \inf_{t} { X_t \in \partial \Omega }.$$

Then we have that

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \int_0^T -\mathcal{L} u (X_t) \, dt + u(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

Compare this with the discrete version of this expression we derived earlier in the tutorial,

$$v(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1}- \mathcal{L} v(X_t) + v(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

We can also define higher dimensional versions of \(X_t\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) instead of in \(\mathbb{R}\). In that case, \(X_t\) is defined as

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t) \cdot dt + \sigma(X_t) \, dB_t^n.$$

In this SDE, \(\mu(x)\) is a vector field on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) determining the drift of the process \(X_t\) when it is at \(x\) and \(\sigma(x)\) determines the volatility (i.e., noise) of the process \(X_t\) when it is at \(x\). \(dB_t^n\) denotes the standard multidimensional infinitesimal Brownian motion, i.e., \(dB_t^n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_n \, dt)\). In this case we get the same expression for \(u(x)\), but the infinitesimal generator becomes

$$\mathcal{L} = \mu(x) \cdot \nabla + \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \, \Delta.$$

Elliptic Partial Differential Equations

We can take the above analysis and use it to get intuition on many of the partial differential equations we are used to seeing in our everyday lives by connecting them to properties of stochastic processes (have you ever read a more ivory tower sentence?). First, let's think of the analogue to our discrete time-game where \(X_t\) wanders around a graph and collects \(f(x)\) reward after leaving vertex \(x\) and \(g(x)\) terminal reward when it reaches a terminal vertex \(x \in D\). In the continuous space/time case, we instead consider \(X_t\) as a stochastic process on a connected bounded domain \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n\). The internal reward function \(f(x)\) is now defined on \(\Omega\) and is interpreted as a rate, i.e., if \(X_t\) is at \(x\) for a period of time \(dt\), then it picks up \(f(x) \, dt\) reward. The terminal reward function \(g(x)\) is now defined on the boundary \(\partial \Omega\). Our expression from the previous section tells us that, if \(u(x)\) solves the elliptic PDE with Dirichlet boundary conditions,

$$\left[\mu(x) \cdot \nabla + \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \, \Delta\right] u(x) = -f(x) \qquad \text{on } \Omega, $$

$$u(x) = g(x) \qquad \text{on } \partial \Omega, $$

then it satisfies

$$ u(x) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \int_0^T -\mathcal{L} u (X_t) \, dt + u(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right] = \mathbb{E} \left[ \int_0^T f(X_t) \, dt + g(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right], $$

where

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t) \cdot dt + \sigma(X_t) \, dB_t^n.$$
This is the probabilistic interpretation of elliptic PDEs.

Note in particular, this gives a nice probabilistic form for the solution of the Laplace equation with Dirichlet boundary conditions, i.e.,

$$ \Delta \, u(x) = 0 \qquad \text{on } \Omega, $$

$$ u(x) = g(x) \qquad \text{on } \partial \Omega, $$

The solution \(u(x)\) satisfies the probabilistic expression

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}[ g(B_T) \mid B_0 = x], $$

where \(B_t\) is a standard Brownian motion. Therefore, the Laplace equation with Dirichlet boundary conditions is the continuous analogue of the expected terminal reward problem we saw earlier. Note moreover, that it is possible to use this expression to evaluate \(u(x)\) with Monte Carlo without solving the entire equation. This is especially useful in high dimensional settings where solving the Laplace equation can be prohibitively expensive.

The Maximum Principle for Elliptic Equations

Another outcome of this probabilistic form is the maximum principle. Suppose that \(\mathcal{L}\) is an infinitesimal generator of some stochastic process \(X_t \in \mathbb{R}^n\), and suppose that the function \(u(x)\) satisfies

$$\mathcal{L} u(x) \geq 0,\qquad \text{on } \Omega.$$

When \(\mathcal{L} = \Delta\), the function \(u(x)\) is called subharmonic on \(\Omega\). If this is the case, then from the probabilistic interpretation of \(u(x)\) in the previous section, we have that

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \int_0^T -\mathcal{L} u (X_t) \, dt + u(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right] \leq \mathbb{E} \left[ u(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right] \leq \sup_{x \in \partial \Omega} u(x).$$

This is the continuous analogue of the discrete maximum principle we saw earlier in the tutorial.

Hitting Time Problems

Suppose that we have a stochastic process \(X_t\) on \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) given by

$$dX_t = \mu(x) \cdot dt + \sigma(x) \, dB_t^n,$$

and we want to ask the continuous analogue of the discrete hitting time problem. That is, how long does it take \(X_t\) to hit the boundary \(\partial \Omega\) if it starts from \(X_0 = x\)? We will write this function as

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[T \mid X_0 = x\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^T \, dt \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

The probabalistic interpretation we derived in the previous sections tells us that \(u(x)\) satisfies the PDE
$$\mu(x) \cdot \nabla u(x) + \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \, \Delta \, u(x) = -1 \qquad \text{on } \Omega,$$
$$u(x) = 0 \qquad \text{on } \partial \Omega.$$

First Arrival Problems

Suppose that we have a stochastic process \(X_t\) on \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) given by

$$dX_t = \mu(x) \cdot dt + \sigma(x) \, dB_t^n,$$

and we want to ask the continuous analogue of the discrete first arrival problem. That is, suppose that \(\partial \Omega\) can be split into two disjoint parts \(\partial\Omega^+\) and \(\partial \Omega^-\). What is the probability that \(X_t\) hits \(\partial \Omega^+\) before it hits \(\partial \Omega^-\) given that it starts at \(X_0 = x\)? Let \(1_{\partial \Omega^+}\) be the indicator function for \(\partial \Omega^+\), then the function we want to compute is

$$u(x) = \mathbb{P}(X_T \in \partial \Omega^+) = \mathbb{E}[1_{\partial \Omega^+}(X_T)].$$

The probabalistic interpretation we derived in the previous sections tells us that \(u(x)\) satisfies the PDE

$$\mu(x) \cdot \nabla u(x) + \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \, \Delta \, u(x) = 0 \qquad \text{on } \Omega, $$

$$u(x) = 1 \qquad \text{on } \partial \Omega^+, $$

$$u(x) = 0 \qquad \text{on } \partial \Omega^-. $$

Parabolic Differential Equations

To move to parabolic differential equations, i.e., time evolution problems, we need a version of Ito's formula for functions for functions \(u(x, t)\) that depend both on space and time. Turns out, adding a time variable doesn't change anything except for adding an extra term in the time partial derivative in \(u(x, t)\). Let's redo the Taylor expansion of \(u(X_t, t)\) in terms of \(dX_t\) and \(dt\),

$$du(X_t, t) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} \, dt + \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \, dX_t + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} , (dX_t)^2$$

Redoing the calculation that we did earlier, this gives us

$$du(X_t, t) = \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} + \mathcal{L}\right) u(X_t) \, dt + \sigma(X_t) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \, dB_t$$

Note that the only thing that has changed is that there is now a time partial derivative in the \(dt\) terms. Since \(dB_t\) is mean zero and independent of everything that happens up until time \(t\), the expectation of \(du\) is given by

$$\mathbb{E}[du(X_t, t) \mid X_0 = x] = \mathbb{E} \left[ \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} + \mathcal{L}\right) u(X_t) \, dt \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

Therefore, if we integrate from time \(0\) to time \(\tau\), we get

$$\mathbb{E}[u(X_\tau, \tau) - u(X_0, 0)\mid X_0 = x] = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_0^\tau \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} + \mathcal{L}\right) u(X_t) \, dt \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

Rearranging the above expression

$$u(x, 0) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_0^\tau \left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}\right) u(X_t) \, dt + u(X_\tau, \tau)\mid X_0 = x\right].$$

Note that this is equivalent to the following more general expression (just shift the result in time):

$$u(x, s) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_s^\tau \left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}\right) u(X_t) \, dt + u(X_\tau, \tau)\mid X_s = x\right].$$

Once again, it is also possible for \(\tau\) to be replaced with a stopping time \(T\) (i.e., the hitting time of the boundary \(\partial \Omega\) of a set, for example), deriving this is beyond the scope of this tutorial, if one assumes some nice properties about the stopping time \(T\) (i.e., finite expectation), we get the most general expression

$$u(x, s) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_s^T \left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}\right) u(X_t) \, dt + u(X_T, T)\mid X_s = x\right].$$

The Backward Kolmogorov Equation

Now that we have derived an expression for \(u(x, s)\) in terms of an average over the paths of a stochastic process \(X_t\), we can use this to derive a continuous time analogue for the backward Kolmogorov equation.

To set the stage, let us suppose again, that we have a stochastic process \(X_t\) given by

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t) \cdot dt + \sigma(X_t) \, dB_t^n.$$

We will look at two versions of this equation. For the first version, suppose that \(X_t\) is free to move on the entirety of \(\mathbb{R}^n\). And suppose we are playing a game where \(X_t\) moves through \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and picks up a reward of \(f(X_t) \, dt\) every time step of \(dt\) for an instantaneous reward function \(f(x, t)\). Like our discrete example, we also suppose that the game only runs for \(\tau\) time and at the end of that time, \(X_t\) picks up a terminal reward of \(g(X_\tau)\) for a terminal reward function \(g(x)\). Then, the quantity of interest is the expected total reward starting at time \(s\) and at location \(X_s = x\), i.e., the value function

$$V(x, s) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_s^\tau f(X_t, t) \, dt + g(X_\tau) \mid X_s = x\right].$$

Note that our probabilistic expression for \(u(x, s)\) in the previous section tells us that \(u(x, s)\) must satisfy the partial differential equation

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = -\mu(x) \cdot \nabla V(x) - \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \, \Delta \, V(x) + f(x, t), \qquad \text{for } t < \tau$$

$$V(x, \tau) = g(x). $$

And vice versa, any \(V\) which satisfies the above partial differential equation has the probabilistic form above. An alternative formulation of the problem is to introduce a domain \(\Omega\) that the process \(X_t\) is allowed to move on until it hits the boundary \(\partial \Omega\) at time \(T\), at which point the game terminates and we receive reward \(h(X_T, T)\). To distinguish this from the elliptic equation case, we introduce an arbitrary cutoff to the game at time \(\tau\), at which point the process \(X_t\) receives the terminal reward \(g(X_\tau)\) if he has not yet terminated. The expected total reward can then be written as

$$V(x, s) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_s^{T \wedge \tau} f(X_t, t) \, dt + 1_{T \leq \tau} h(X_T, T) + 1_{T > \tau} g(X_\tau) \mid X_s = x\right],$$

where \(T \wedge \tau\) denotes the minimum of \(T\) and \(\tau\) (coincidentally \(T \wedge \tau\) is also a stopping time), and \(1_A\) denotes the indicator function for the event \(A\) (i.e., \(1\) when \(A\) occurs and \(0\) otherwise). Suppose that \(u(x, t)\) takes value \(h(x, t)\) on \(\partial \Omega\) and takes value \(g(x)\) at time \(\tau\). Then the above expression can be rewritten as

$$V(x, s) = \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_s^{T \wedge \tau} f(X_t, t) \, dt + V(X_{T \wedge \tau}, T \wedge \tau) \mid X_s = x\right].$$

Therefore, our probabilistic expression from the previous section tells us that \(V\) must satisfy the partial differential equation

$$ \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = -\mu(x) \cdot \nabla V(x) - \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \, \Delta \, V(x) + f(x, t), \qquad \text{for } x \in \Omega, t < \tau, $$

$$V(x, \tau) = g(x), \qquad \,$$

$$V(x, t) = h(x, t), \qquad \text{for x} \in \partial \Omega.$$

This is the backward Kolmogorov equation with Dirichlet boundary conditions.

The Forward Kolmogorov Equation / Advection-Diffusion Equation

We have so far seen how we can compute value functions for stochastic processes \(X_t\). But, another key feature of stochastic processes which is important is their distributions \(\pi_t\) at time \(t\), starting from an initial distribution \(X_0 \sim \pi_0\) (potentially supported at a single point). We would like to write down an equation for how the distribution \(\pi_t\) evolves in time.

For clarity of notation let us write \(\pi_t\) as a function \(\pi(x, t)\). Note that it is actually a series of distributions on the domain of \(X_t\), so this is somewhat of an abuse of notation, but this technicality will not change the following results.

To begin the process of deriving the equation for \(\pi(x, t)\), we will first derive the weak form of the evolution equation for \(\pi\). Consider the parabolic domain \(\mathbb{R}^n \times [0, \tau]\) of the process \(X_t\) and let \(\phi(x, t) \in \mathcal{C}_0^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n \times [0, \tau])\) be an arbitrary test function on this domain that satisfies \(\phi(x, 0) = \phi(x, \tau) = 0\). Recall the probabilistic expression from earlier,

$$\phi(x, 0) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \int_0^\tau \left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}\right) \phi(X_t, t) \, dt + \phi(X_\tau, \tau) \right] .$$

Note that because \(\phi\) satisfies the boundary conditions \(\phi(x, 0) = \phi(x, \tau) = 0\), the above expression simplifies to

$$\mathbb{E} \left[ \int_0^\tau \left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}\right) \phi(X_t, t) \, dt\right] = 0,$$

which can be written as

$$\int_0^\tau \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \pi(x, t) \, \left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}\right) \phi(x, t) \, dx \, dt = 0$$

This is the weak form of the forward Kolmogorov equation (sometimes called the Fokker-Planck equation). The distribution \(\pi_t\) must satisfy the above weak PDE. To obtain the strong form of the PDE, we must assume that \(\pi_t\) is once differentiable in time and twice differentiable in space. If we do this, we can integrate the above expression by parts to get

$$\int_0^\tau \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \phi(x, t) \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}^*\right) \, \pi(x, t) \, dx \, dt = 0,$$

where the boundary terms vanish because \(\phi\) is compactly supported and vanishes when \(t = 0\) or \(t = \tau\), and the operator \(\mathcal{L}^\) is the adjoint of \(\mathcal{L}\) (think of it like the matrix tranpose). The operator \(\mathcal{L}^\) can be written out as

$$\mathcal{L}^* \pi = - \nabla \cdot (\mu(x) \pi(x,t)) + \frac{1}{2} \Delta (\sigma^2(x) \pi(x,t)).$$

Note that there is a negative sign on the \(\nabla\), but there is not a negative sign on the \(\Delta\). This comes from the fact that \(\Delta\) is integrated by parts twice, whereas \(\nabla\) is integrated only once. Now, since it is the case that this must be true for any such test function \(\phi\), it must be that

$$\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}^*\right) \phi(x, t) = 0$$

everywhere. Written in other words,

$$\frac{\partial \pi_t}{\partial t} = - \nabla \cdot (\mu(x) \pi_t(x)) + \frac{1}{2} \Delta (\sigma^2(x) \pi_t(x)).$$

We have therefore derived the evolution equation for the distribution \(\pi_t\) of the process \(X_t\), sometimes called the Fokker-Planck equation. It is also sometimes written in the form

$$\frac{\partial \pi_t}{\partial t} = - \nabla \cdot (\mu(x) \pi_t(x)) + \nabla \cdot ( a(x) \nabla \pi_t(x)),$$

and in this form, it is called the advection-diffusion equation and \(a(x)\) is called the coefficient of diffusivity.

One special case is the heat equation

$$\frac{\partial \pi_t}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2} \Delta \pi_t,$$

which describes the time evolution of the probability distribution of a standard Brownian motion \(B_t^n\) on \(\mathbb{R}^n\). This fact is the reason why the heat equation (and moreover, any forward Kolmogorov equation) preserves the energy functional given by

$$\mathcal{E}_t(\pi) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \pi(x, t) \, dx,$$

as since \(\pi(x, t)\) is a probability distribution for fixed \(t\), \(\mathcal{E}_t(\pi)\) must always evaluate to one.

The Heisenberg Picture

There is a nice way to think of the duality between the forwards and backwards equations is known in physics as the Heisenberg picture. Suppose we have a function \(g(x)\) on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and we want to evaluate the expectation of \(g(X_t)\) at time \(t\), i.e.,

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X_t)] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} g(x) \pi_t(x) \, dx = \langle g, \pi_t \rangle_{L^2}. $$

As we saw before, the evolution of the distribution \(\pi_t\) is given by the equation

$$\frac{\partial \pi_t}{\partial t} = \mathcal{L}^* \pi_t. $$

Therefore, it is common for us to write the action of time evolution for a duration \(t\) under the above equation as being a linear operator denoted by \(e^{t \mathcal{L}^* }\) -- as this would be the solution to the above equation if this were a system of ODEs instead of a PDE. It turns out that taking the adjoint of an exponential of an operator is equivalent to taking the exponential of the adjoint, i.e.,

$$\left(e^{t \mathcal{L}^* }\right)^* = e^{t \mathcal{L}}$$

Thus, we have that

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X_t)] = \langle g, \pi_t \rangle_{L^2} = \langle g, e^{t\mathcal{L}^*} \pi_0 \rangle_{L^2} = \langle e^{t\mathcal{L}} g, \pi_0 \rangle_{L^2} = \mathbb{E}[(e^{t\mathcal{L}}) g(X_0)].$$

This is saying that we can either compute the expectation by evolving the distribution \(\pi_t\) forward in time and taking the inner product with \(g\), or by evolving the function \(g\) backward in time and taking the inner product with \(\pi_0\). Hence, the names of the Kolmogorov forward and backward equations.

This might be a bit abstract, but to put yourself on solid ground, it is worth thinking about the discrete analogue on a graph. Recall that if \(g(x)\) is a function on the vertices of graph, and we have a Markov process \(X_t\) with probability transition matrix \(P\) (i.e., applying \(P^T\) to a distribution evolves it forward in time), then the expectation of \(g(X_t)\) is given by
$$\mathbb{E}[g(X_t)] = g^T \pi_t = g^T (P^T)^t \pi_0 = (P^t g)^T \pi_0 = \mathbb{E}[(P^t g)(X_0)].$$

We see that by either choosing to group the time evolution operator \(P^T\) with the probability distribution \(\pi_0\) or the test function \(g\) we either evolve the probability distribution forward in time or the test function \(g\) backward in time (via \(P\)).

In short, the forward Kolmogorov equation evolves distributions forward in time, while the backward Kolmogorov equation evolves test functions backward in time.

Time Reversal and the Feynman Interpretation

A way to put the backward Kolmogorov equation into a more palatable form is by time reversing it. Let us denote \(q = -t\). Then, we have that

$$dq = - dt.$$

And we also have that

$$dB_q^n = - dB_t^n,$$

It is well known that a Brownian motion viewed in reverse has the same statistics as a regular Brownian motion (think of it like how when you flip a normal distribution, the result is still normal), i.e. Brownian motion has time reversal symmetry, and the above equation is how we express that fact. Now, we can write the infinitesimal increment \(dX_t\) in terms of the reverse time variable \(q\),

$$dX_q =_d - \mu(X_q) \cdot dq - \sigma(X_q) \, dB_q^n.$$

This tell us one very important fact - the infinitesimal generator of \(X_t\) under time reversal is equal to

$$\mathcal{L}_{-t} = - \mu(x) \cdot \nabla + \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \Delta,$$

Now finally, this brings us to the Feynman interpretation of the backward Kolmogorov / heat equation. Suppose we have the equation

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \mu(x) \cdot \nabla u(x) + \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \, \Delta \, V(x) + f(x, t), \qquad \text{for } t > 0, $$
$$V(x, 0) = g(x), $$

Our derivations in the section about the backward Kolmogorov equation tells us that \(u(x, t)\) can be viewed as the expectation
$$u(x, t) = \mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^t f(X_s, s) \, ds + g(X_0) \mid X_t = x\right],$$

where \(X_t\) is the stochastic process given by
$$dX_t = \mu(X_t) \cdot dt + \sigma(X_t) \, dB_t^n.$$

We can therefore think of the forward equation as performing a weighted sum over all paths the process \(X_t\) can take to reach location \(x\) and \(t\), where the weight of a specific instance of the path is proportional to \(\int_0^t f(X_t, t) \, dt + g(X_0)\). You may have heard of the famous Feynman integral from quantum mechanics, this is a close cousin of that integral (in fact, the Feynman integral is this integral extended to imaginary values of time). The idea is you can evaluate \(u(x, t)\) by summing over all paths that \(X_t\) can take to get to \(x\) at time \(t\). Alternatively, we can think of the above expression in its time-reversed form, i.e., instead of using \(X_t\), we time reverse \(X_t\) to get the process \(Y_t\), given by

$$dY_t = -\mu(X_t) \cdot dt - \sigma(X_t) \, dB_t^n,$$

and we can evaluate

$$u(x, t) = \mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^t f(Y_s, t - s) \, ds + g(Y_t) \mid Y_0 = x\right].$$

This expression can be very useful, since it allows us to evaluate the solution of the heat equation at a single point \((x, t)\) with Monte Carlo without having to rely on solving the heat equation everywhere, which can be prohibitively expensive in high dimensions.

Similarly, the heat equation with Dirichlet boundary conditions, i.e.,

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \mu(x) \cdot \nabla u(x) + \frac{1}{2} \sigma(x)^2 \, \Delta \, u(x) + f(x, t), \qquad \text{for } x \in \Omega, t > 0,$$

$$u(x, t) = h(x, t), \qquad \text{for x} \in \partial \Omega \text{ and } t = 0.$$

has the probabilistic interpretation

$$ u(x, t) = \mathbb{E} \left[\int_0^{t \wedge T} f(Y_s, t - s) \, ds + h(Y_{t \wedge T}, s - t \wedge T) \mid Y_0 = x\right],$$

where \(T\) is the hitting time of the boundary. Here, paths may terminate before time \(t\) if they hit the boundary \(\partial \Omega\), if they do, the weight of the path is \(\int_0^{T} f(Y_s, t - s) \, ds + h(Y_{T}, T)\) otherwise the weight of the path is \(\int_0^{t} f(Y_s, t - s) \, ds + h(Y_{t}, 0)\).

The Maximum Principle for Parabolic Equations

A cool corollary of the above probabilistic expression is the maximum principle for parabolic equations. Suppose that \(u(x, t)\) is defined on \(\mathbb{R}^n \times [0, \tau]\) and satisfies

$$\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L} \right) u \leq 0,$$

for the infinitesimal generator \(\mathcal{L}\) of some stochastic process \(X_t\). Then it follows that

$$u(x, t) = \mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^t \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L} \right) u(Y_s, t - s) \, ds + u(Y_t, 0) \mid Y_0 = x\right] \leq \mathbb{E}[u(Y_t, 0) \mid Y_0 = x] \leq \sup_{y} u(y, 0) .$$

Likewise, if \(u(x, t)\) is defined on a domain \(\Omega \times [0, \tau]\), then we have

$$u(x, t) \leq \max \left( \sup_{y \in \partial \Omega, s \in [0, \tau]} u(y, s), \qquad \sup_{y \in \Omega} u(y, 0) \right).$$

Stationary Distribution Problems

Like in the discrete case, the distribution \(\pi_t\) of a stochastic process \(X_t\) with nonzero diffusion (i.e., \(\sigma > 0\)) always approaches a stationary distribution \(\pi\) as \(t \rightarrow \infty\) (we won't show this here, but it's true). To compute what this stationary distribution is, we take a look at the evolution equation for \(\pi_t\), i.e.,

$$\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} - \mathcal{L}^*\right) \pi_t = 0 .$$

For the change in \(\pi\) to be zero, therefore, we must have that the stationary distribution satisfies the elliptic equation

$$\mathcal{L}^* \pi = 0.$$

Written out in full,

$$-\nabla \cdot (\mu(x) \pi(x)) + \frac{1}{2} \Delta (\sigma(x)^2 \pi(x)) = 0.$$

Naturally, one must also enforce the constraint that

$$\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \pi(x) \, dx = 1,$$

to ensure the result is actually a probability distribution.

Why You Should Care: Sampling with Langevin Dynamics

Langevin dynamics is an important tool in computational science for sampling from distributions on \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Imagine that we are given a probability distribution \(p(x)\) such that we can evaluate the gradient of its logarithm \(\nabla \log p(x)\). \(p(x)\) may be the posterior of a statistical model after it has been trained on data, for example. Often times, in these statistics settings, evaluating \(p(x)\) is hard, but \(\nabla \log p(x)\) can be evaluated easily. Another common situation where one wants to sample from a distribution is in statistical physics, where the equilibrium distributions of systems is often times known and one is interested in computing properties of the system.

Sometimes we might want to evaluate the mode of \(p(x)\) (i.e., compute a maximum likelihood estimator). This is often used by using gradient descent, whose continuous time analogue can be written as

$$dX_t = \nabla \log p(x) \, dt.$$

If \(X_t\) is allowed to run long enough, eventually \(X_t\) will converge to a local mode of the distribution \(p(x)\). However, often times the mode of a distribution is not a good representation of the distribution as a whole, and we would rather have the ability to sample from it. To see how this can be done, consider the above equation, but with a bit of noise added from Brownian motion,

$$dX_t = \nabla \log p(x) \, dt + \sqrt{2} \, dB_t.$$

This process is referred to as overdamped Langevin dynamics. Let's compute the stationary distribution of this process using the machinery from the previous section. The stationary distribution \(\pi\) should satisfy

$$-\nabla \cdot ( \pi(x) \nabla \log p(x)) + \Delta \pi(x) = 0.$$

We can rearrange this to get

$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \pi(x) - \pi(x) \nabla \log p(x)) = 0.$$

Therefore,

$$\nabla \pi(x) - \pi(x) \nabla \log p(x) = \phi,$$

where \(\phi\) is a divergence free vector field (i.e., \(\nabla \cdot \phi = 0\)). However, note that since \(\pi\) is a probability distribution, it must vanish as \(x \rightarrow \infty\). There is no everywhere differentiable divergence free vector field which vanishes as \(x \rightarrow \infty\). Therefore \(\phi = 0\) and

$$\nabla \pi(x) - \pi(x) \nabla \log p(x) = 0.$$

Note that there is always a nonzero probability that \(X_t\) ends up at any given location \(x\), so therefore, \(\pi(x) > 0\) and we can divided by \(\pi(x)\) to get

$$\frac{\nabla \pi(x)}{\pi(x)} = \nabla \log p(x)$$

But the left hand side is just

$$\nabla \log \pi(x) = \nabla \log p(x).$$

which means

$$\log \pi(x) = \log p(x) + C$$

Or rather,

$$\pi(x) = A p(x)$$

For some constant \(A\). But integrating both sides gives

$$\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \pi(x) \, dx = A \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} p(x) \, dx.$$

And since both \(\pi(x)\) and \(p(x)\) are probability distributions, their integrals evaluate to one and we therefore have \(A = 1\) and

$$\pi(x) = p(x).$$

Therefore, the stationary distribution of \(X_t\) is precisely the distribution \(p(x)\). This means we can draw a sample from \(p(x)\) by sampling \(X_0\) from some arbitrary distribution (preferably one already close to \(p(x)\) if we can) and letting \(X_t\) run for a long time. After this burn in period, we examine \(X_t\) and the result is an approximate sample from \(p(x)\). This is an example of a Markov Chain Monte Carlo technique.

Introduction

Hitting Time Problems on the Path Graph

Let us consider the simplest case of an elliptic system - namely an elliptic system on the path graph \(P_N\) with \(N\) vertices, labelled \(0,...,N-1\). Let us define a random process \(X_t \in V(P_N)\) for \(t \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}\) as a random walk on the graph \(P_N\) (i.e., the process \(X_t\) moves left or right with equal probability, unless it is at the boundary, where it bounces back).

A natural question to ask about the process \(X_t\) is, given that \(X_t\) starts at a certain location (say, \(X_0 = n\)), how long will it take the process \(X_t\) to hit either the left or right boundary of the graph? For some visual flair, maybe imagine that \(X_t\) is a drunk guy who is trying to get home and is stumbling back on forth on a circular driveway (the endpoints of \(P_N\) are his home) -- and we want to ask how long on average it will take the drunk man to get home?

This question turns out to essentially be a linear system. To see why, let's define the stopping time \(T\) to be the first time that \(X_t\) arrives either at \(0\) or \(N - 1\). The above problem is asking us to compute the expected value of \(T\), i.e., compute the function

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}[ T | X_0 = x].$$

A linear system for \(u(x)\) can be expressed using a technique known as first transition analysis. First transition analysis expands the above expression by considering all of the possible transitions that the process \(X_t\) can make starting at \(X_0 = x\). We know that \(X_t\) can go left or right with probability \(1/2\), therefore by the properties of conditional expectation, we have that

$$u(x) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbb{E}[ T | X_1 = x - 1] + \frac{1}{2} \mathbb{E}[ T | X_1 = x + 1].$$

However, note that the process \(X_t\) is Markovian, that is, it has no memory of the past. What the process \(X_t\) does at time \(t + 1\) doesn't dependent on anything except for the state of \(X_t\) at time \(t\). Therefore, it follows that

$$\mathbb{E}[ T | X_1 = y] = \mathbb{E}[ T | X_0 = y] + 1.$$

In English, this mathematical statement is expressing the fact that expected time until \(X_t\) hits the boundary if it is at state \(y\) at time \(1\) is the same as the expected time for \(X_t\) hits the boundary starting had it started from \(y\) originally, plus one (because we have effectively rewound the clock by one unit of time and need to account for this). Thus, we can plug this into the first transition analysis expression for \(u(x)\) to get

$$u(x) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbb{E}[ T | X_0 = x - 1] + \frac{1}{2} \mathbb{E}[ T | X_0 = x + 1] + 1 = \frac{1}{2} u(x - 1) + \frac{1}{2} u(x + 1) + 1.$$

Rearranging,

$$-\frac{1}{2} u(x - 1) + u(x) - \frac{1}{2} u(x + 1) = 1.$$

Note however, that this is only valid for the interior of the path graph \(P_N\) and therefore, we need to apply boundary conditions at \(x = 0\) and \(x = N - 1\). This is fairly easy, because if the process starts at either of the ends of the path, then the expected amount of time until it reaches the ends of the graph should be zero,

$$u(0) = 0,$$

$$u(N - 1) = 0.$$

If we write out what the matrix for this system, we see it has the form

$$ \frac{1}{2} \left[\begin{array}{cccccc} 2 & -1 & & & & \\ -1 & 2 & -1 & & \\ & -1 & 2 & -1 & & \\ & & & \ddots & & \\ & & -1 & 2 & -1 \\ & & & -1 & 2 \end{array}\right] \left[\begin{array}{c} u(1) \\ u(2) \\ u(3) \\ \vdots \\ u(N-3) \\ u(N-2) \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{c} 1\\ 1 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right] $$

You might recognize the matrix above as the second order finite difference stencil matrix for the Poisson equation in 1D. As we will see later, this is not entirely a coincidence, as the Poisson equation has a similar probabilistic interpretation.

Elliptic Problems on Graphs

Hitting Time Problems on General Graphs

Now that we've seen a basic elliptic system arrise out a hitting time problem for a stochastic process on the path graph \(P_N\), it is time to move to the more general types of graphs. Suppose we have a graph \(G = (V, E)\) and we have a stochastic process \(X_t \in V\) defined on the vertex set \(V\). If we have a special set of death vertices \(D \subset V\), we can once again ask the analogue of the above hitting time question: how long does \(X_t\) take to hit a vertex in \(D\), given that it starts at \(x \in V\)? Once, again, we want to compute

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}[T \mid X_0 = x],$$

where \(T\) is the hitting time of \(D\), i.e.,

$$T = \inf_{t} { X_t \in D }.$$

If we only consider \(X_t\) which are Markovian, the dynamics of \(X_t\) can be characterized completely by its probability transition kernel \(P\) (also known as the transition matrix), defined by

$$P(x,y) = \mathbb{P}(X_t = y \mid X_{t - 1} = x),$$

i.e., the probability that we make the transition \(x \rightarrow y\) if we are at vertex \(x\). If we do first transition analysis on the process \(X_t\), we can write down an expression for \(u(x)\) like in the previous section,

$$u(x) = \sum_v P(x,v) \, u(v) + 1.$$

Once, again rearranging this expression yields

$$u(x) - \sum_v P(x,v) \, u(v) = 1.$$

This equation is often written as

$$\mathcal{L}\, u = -1,$$

where

$$\mathcal{L} = P - I$$

is known as the generator of the process \(X_t\). You may recognize this matrix from spectral graph theory, where it is the negative of the graph Laplacian.

To get a well-posed system of equations for \(u\), we must also enforce boundary conditions, namely,

$$u(x) = 0 \qquad \text{for } x \in D.$$

Together this gives us the complete elliptic system of equations for the hitting time of \(D\) from anywhere in the graph, which we can write down as

$$ \mathcal{L} \, u = -1, \qquad \text{on } V \setminus D$$

$$u = 0, \qquad \text{on } D. $$

A very nice thing about these types of problems is that they can be solved in nearly linear time by Laplacian system solvers (as mentioned in CME305).

However, there are many other types of interesting problems concerning stochastic processes which also reduce to elliptic systems. We will examine an example in the next section.

Expected Terminal Reward Problems on Graphs

Suppose instead of being interested in the hitting time of a set \(D \subset V\), we are instead interested in some kind of terminal reward on \(D\). Denote this reward as a function \(g : D \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}\). Maybe think of this like \(X_t\) is a person wandering through a maze until he gets to the an exit \(v \in D\), whereupon he recieves the reward \(g(v)\). A question we can ask about \(X_t\) is the expected terminal reward that the process \(X_t\) recieves given \(X_t\) starts at a vertex \(x\), i.e.

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}[g(X_T) \mid X_0 = x].$$

Turns out we can use first transition analysis here too! Let's try it,

$$u(x) = \sum_v \mathbb{P}(X_1 = v \mid X_0 = x)\mathbb{E}[g(X_T) \mid X_1 = v]$$

Now, note that by the Markov property,

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X_T) \mid X_1 = v] = \mathbb{E}[g(X_T) \mid X_0 = v],$$

because the expected terminal reward is the same if \(X_t\) starts at \(v\) or if we know that \(X_t\) transitioned to \(v\) on the first time step. Note we don't have to add one in this case like we did for the hitting time problem --- this is because the value of \(T\) does not impact the terminal reward. Therefore,

$$u(x) = \sum_v P(x, v) \, u(v),$$

which can be written as

$$\mathcal{L} \, u = 0, \qquad \text{on } V \setminus D$$

However, we now need different boundary conditions. But, note that if \(X_t\) starts on a vertex \(x \in D\), then we immediately terminate and receive a reward of \(g(x)\). Therefore, we have that

$$u = g , \qquad \text{on }D,$$

this is sometimes called a Laplace equation with Dirichlet boundary conditions.

First Arrival Problems

An interesting subset of the above collection of problems are first arrival problems. Suppose the termination set \(D \subset V\) is split into two disjoint subsets \(A \cup B = D\). What is the probability that, when we terminate, it is because we are in \(A\)? Another way of phrasing this problem, is what is the probability that \(X_t\) hits \(A\) before it hits \(B\)?. It turns out that solving this problem is very easy with the above machinery. Simply define \(g\) to be \(1\) on \(A\) and \(0\) on \(B\). Then, the expected reward is precisely the probability that we hit \(A\) before \(B\).

The system of equations written out in full is

$$ \mathcal{L} \, u = 0, \qquad \text{on } V \setminus D, $$
$$ u = 1, \qquad \text{on } A,$$
$$u = 0, \qquad \text{on } B $$

General Discounted Reward Problems

The most general form of an elliptic system arrising from a stochastic process on a graph can be phrased in terms of discounted reward. Suppose, like above that \(X_t\) wanders through the graph playing the above game until it arrives at a vertex in \(D\) (this time though, we allow \(D = \emptyset\), i.e., the game continues forever). When \(X_t\) arrives at a terminal vertex in \(x \in D\), the game stops and \(X_t\) receives a reward \(g(x)\). However, along the way, the process \(X_t\) also receives an ongoing stream of rewards depending on the vertices it visits. That is, if \(X_t = x\), then the process receives \(f(x)\) reward at time \(t\) where \(f\) is defined on the non-terminal vertices, i.e. \(f : V \setminus D \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}\). This problem revolves around the expected total reward received over the course of the game,

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1} f(X_t) + g(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

In general settings, one may also consider discounted reward, where the reward received at time \(t\) is discounted by a factor of \(0 \leq \gamma \leq 1\). This means that reward collected at the beginning of the game is more important than reward received later on (this is a common situation in economics or finance, where having money in the bank can net you interest, so one dollar today is worth more than one dollar a year from now). Then, the total discounted reward reads

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1} \gamma^t f(X_t) + \gamma^T g(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

How do we solve for \(u(x)\)? You guessed it! First transition analysis! Here we go:

$$ \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1} \gamma^t f(X_t) + \gamma^T g(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right] $$

$$= \sum_v P(v, x) \, \left(\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 1}^{T - 1} \gamma^t f(X_t) + \gamma^T g(X_T) \mid X_1 = v\right] + f(x)\right)$$

$$\qquad= f(x) + \gamma\sum_v P(x, v) \, \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 2} \gamma^t f(X_{t + 1}) + \gamma^{T - 1} g(X_{T}) \mid X_1 = v\right]$$

$$\qquad= f(x) + \gamma\sum_v P(x, v) \, \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1} \gamma^t f(X_{t}) + \gamma^{T} g(X_{T}) \mid X_0 = v\right]$$

$$\qquad= f(x) + \gamma \sum_v P(x, v) \, u(v),$$

where from line 1 we used the fact that the process receives \(f(x)\) reward and then transitions to a new state \(v\) with probability \(P(x, v)\), and from line 2 to line 3 we used the Markov property of \(X_t\). Therefore, on \(V \setminus D\), we have

$$u(x) - \gamma \sum_v P(x, v) \, u(v) = f(x),$$

and on \(D\), we have

$$u(x) = g(x).$$

In matrix form, this can be written as

$$ (I - \gamma P) u = f, \qquad \text{on } V \setminus D, $$

$$ u = g, \qquad \text{on } D. $$

Therefore, if you ever see a linear system of the above form on a graph (i.e., a Laplacian system with discounting), where \(P\) is a transition matrix, you can always interpret the result with the expression

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1} \gamma^t f(X_t) + \gamma^T g(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right],$$

where the inhomogeneous term \(f(x)\) is the reward received for leaving vertex \(x\), the Dirichlet boundary condition \(g(x)\) is the terminal reward received when the game ends as \(X_t\) arrives at \(x\), and \(\gamma\) is the discount factor of this game. Note that if the termination set \(D\) is empty, then the game runs forever (for the total reward to be finite the discount factor must be \(0 \leq \gamma <1\) in this case), and the expression for reward is given by

$$u(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{\infty} \gamma^t f(X_t) \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

An immediate consequence of these probabilistic expressions is the maximal principle,

Discrete Maximum Principle for Subharmonic Functions

Functions \(v\) defined on the vertices of a graph \(G\) with a stochastic process \(X_t\) with generator \(\mathcal{L}\) are called subharmonic on \(A \subset V\) if
$$\mathcal{L} \, v \geq 0, \qquad \text{on } A.$$
(It is harmonic if \(\mathcal{L} \, v = 0\)). Note that any \(v\) is trivially the solution to the system
$$(I - P) \, v = -\mathcal{L} \, v, \qquad \text{on } A,$$
$$v = v, \qquad \text{on } \partial A,$$
where we define \(\partial A\) as the set of vertices that not in \(A\), but are adjacent to a vertex in \(A\). Therefore, the probabilistic interpretation we've developed tells us that, for \(x \in A\),

$$v(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1}- \mathcal{L} v(X_t) + v(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right] \leq \mathbb{E}[v(X_T)] \leq \max_{y \in \partial A} v(y),$$

where \(T\) is the hitting time of \(\partial A\). Note that we have used the fact that \(\mathcal{L} v \leq 0\) to drop the \(\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1}(\mathcal{L} v)(X_t)\) term and then used the fact that \(X_T \in \partial A\).

Therefore, if \(v\) is a subharmonic function on a domain \(A\) in the graph \(G\), its maximum must always occur on the boundary of \(A\). This is fundamental result in analysis (for subharmonic functions on \(\mathbb{R}^n\)), but this probabilistic interpretation of the Laplacian gives us a super easy way of seeing why the result is true.

A byproduct of this analysis is that if the function \(v(x)\) is actually harmonic, instead of just subharmonic, we have that, for \(x \in A\),
$$v(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[v(X_T) \mid X_0 = x\right] = \sum_{y \in \partial A} \mathbb{P}(X_T = y) \, v(y). $$
Thus, \(v(x)\) is essentially just a weighted average over the boundary \(\partial A\), where the contribution of \(v(y)\) depends on the probability of the walk \(X_t\) ending up at \(y\) when it terminates.

This idea of expressing a function in terms of an expectation of a function of stochastic process \(X_t\) gives us a glimpse of a fundamental technique in stochastic differential equations called Ito's formula (to be talked about later).

Stationary Distribution Problems

Another important property is the stationary distribution of stochastic processes \(X_t\) on graphs. Stationary distributions are extremely important in many data science applications. For example, one way to rank the relative importance of web domains on the internet is to think about a random walk \(X_t\) through the internet and ask the question: after a very long time what is the average amount of time that \(X_t\) has spent at a specific webpage? It turns out this question has a unique answer (for most graphs) that doesn't depend on the starting point of \(X_t\). Indeed, the average fraction of the time \(X_t\) spends at \(x\) (over an infinitely long time) is given by the entries of a special distribution \(\pi\) called the stationary distribution. I won't go into much detail here, but basically if \(\pi_t\) denotes the probability distribution of \(X_t\) at time \(t\) on the graph, then \(\pi\) is the limit of the \(\pi_t\) as \(t \rightarrow \infty\).

The key property which defines the stationary distribution is that if \(X_0\) has distribution \(\pi\), i.e., \(X_0 \sim \pi\), then \(X_1, X_2, X_3 ...\), etc. also have distribution \(\pi\). Hence, why \(\pi\) is called stationary. To write down an equation for the stationary distribution, we should first write down how the distributions \(\pi_t\) evolve with time. This turns out not to be to difficult, as to compute \(\pi_t(x)\) we simply need to some over all of the possible ways \(X_{t - 1}\) can reach \(x\) and weigh those quantities by the probability of making the transition \(X_{t - 1} \rightarrow x\),

$$\pi_t(x) = \sum_{v} \mathbb{P}(X_t = x \mid X_{t - 1} = v) \, \mathbb{P}(X_{t - 1} = v) = \sum_v P(v, x) \, \pi_{t - 1}(v).$$

In matrix form, this is given by

$$\pi_{t} = P^T \pi_{t - 1}.$$

Note the transpose of the probability transition kernel has been used, we will remark on this in the subsequent section when we come to parabolic problems on graphs. In the mean time, we remark that the stationary distribution should therefore satisfy

$$\pi = P^T \pi.$$

Rewriting this gives us

$$\mathcal{L}^T \pi = 0.$$

Note that this equation is actually underdetermined because the transpose generator \(\mathcal{L}^T\) is not full rank (you can check that \(\mathcal{L} 1 = 0\)). This problem can be solved by adding the additional constraint that the entries of \(\pi\) should sum to \(1\),

$$\sum_x \pi(x) = 1$$

If the graph is aperiodic and connected, these system of equations will give the unique stationary distribution (see fundamental theorem of Markov chains).

Discrete-Time Parabolic Problems on Graphs

Parabolic problems usually concern the time evolution of properties of stochastic processes \(X_t\) on a graph \(G = (V, E)\). Turns out we have actually already examined one example of a parabolic problem on a graph, namely the evolution of the distribution \(\pi_t\) of \(X_t\) with time \(t\). The equation that governs this evolution is usually called the forward Kolmogorov equation or the heat equation

The Forward Kolmogorov Equation / Heat Equation

Let us recall from the previous section that the equation governing the evolution of the probability distributions \(\pi_t\) is given by

$$\pi_{t + 1} = P^T \pi_{t}.$$

By subtracting \(\pi_{t}\) from both sides, we arrive at

$$\pi_{t + 1} - \pi_t = (P^T - I) \pi_t,$$

which can be rewritten as

$$\Delta_t \pi_t = \mathcal{L}^T \, \pi_t,$$

where \(\mathcal{L}\) is the generator of \(X_t\) (i.e., the graph Laplacian) and \(\Delta_t\) is the discrete time derivative. This is the discrete-time forward Kolmogorov equation or the discrete-time heat equation. Note the similarity of this equation to the continuous heat equation,

$$\frac{\partial \pi_t}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2} \Delta \, \pi_t.$$

However, there are some notable differences between the discrete and continuous heat equations. These are mostly just a matter of convention. There is a transpose because of the way we defined \(P\). The factor of \(\frac{1}{2}\) is also to some extent as result of convention, but it is harder to explain away for now. Morally, you should think of \(\mathcal{L}\) as the discrete analogue of \(\frac{1}{2} \Delta\) (note the factor of \(\frac{1}{2}\) that appeared in the introduction section). Indeed, on grid graphs, an appropriately normalized \(\mathcal{L}^T = \mathcal{L}\) (\(\mathcal{L}\) is symmetric on grid graphs) will "converge" to \(\frac{1}{2} \Delta\) as the grid becomes infinitely fine. This bridge allows us to translate intuition between the worlds discrete spectral graph theory and continuous PDEs.

The Backward Kolmogorov Equation / Backward Heat Equation

The Kolmogorov Backward Equation arises from a similar setup to the discounted reward problem. Let us modify the discounted reward problem a bit to turn it into a time evolution problem. Suppose we have a similar setup as before: i.e., we have a process \(X_t\) on the graph \(G = (V, E)\). However, this time, the reward function \(f(x)\) is allowed to be time dependent, so it is a function both of a vertex \(x\) and a time \(t\). Suppose, that we let the process \(X_t \in V\) run on the graph until time \(\tau \in \mathbb{N}\), and at every time step, it collects \(f(X_t)\) reward until time \(\tau\), when it collects \(g(X_\tau)\) terminal reward and the game ends. Note that unlike \(T\) from the previous section, which is a stopping time, and may be random, \(\tau\) is simply a constant that we choose before hand. Now, we ask the question: given we start the game with \(X_0 = x\), what is the expect amount of (discounted) reward that \(X_t\) accumulates in time up to \(\tau\)? That is, we want to compute the value function of the state \(x\),

$$V(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = 0}^{\tau - 1} \gamma^t f(x, t) + g(X_\tau) \mid X_0 = x\right].$$

One way to solve this problem is by recursion. Let us extend the value function to starting times \(s > 0\). We do this as follows:

$$V(x, s)= \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = s}^\tau \gamma^{t - s} f(x, t) + g(X_\tau)\mid X_s = x\right],$$

That is, \(V(x, s)\) is the expected reward collected if we start at time \(s\) and at state \(x\). We can use first transition analysis to write down an equation for this function that we can then solve,

$$ V(x, s) = f(X_s, s) + \gamma \sum_v P(x, v) \, \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t = s + 1}^{\tau - 1} \gamma^{t - s - 1} f(x, t) \mid X_{s + 1} = v\right] $$

$$= f(X_s, s) + \gamma \sum_v P(x, v) \, V(v, s + 1) . $$

Note that the above is a linear system for the \(\{ V(x, s) \}_{x \in V}\) in terms of the \(\{ V(x, s+1) \}_{x \in V}\). Therefore, since we have the boundary condition

$$V(x, t) = g(x),$$

we can recursively solve for the \(V(x, s)\) backwards in time until we get the \(V(x, 0)\) we originally desired. Because we are solve backwards in time, this is referred to as the backward Kolmogorov equation or backward heat equation. Let's rewrite the system of equations to put it into a more (subjectively) digestable form,

$$V(x, s + 1) - V(x, s) = \left(V(x, s + 1) - \gamma \sum_v P(x, v) \, V(v, s + 1)\right) - f(X_s, s).$$

Note we have simply multiplied both sides by \(-1\) and added \(V(x, s + 1)\) to both sides. Note that the left hand side of the equation is essentially a time derivative (as it is the change in \(V(x)\) from time \(s\) to time \(s + 1\)). Thus, we can write the whole equation above in matrix form as

$$\Delta_t V_t = (I - \gamma P) V_{t + 1} - f_t.$$

Where \(\Delta_t\) is the discrete time derivative. When the discount factor \(\gamma\) is identity, we get

$$\Delta_t V_t = -\mathcal{L} \, V_{t + 1} - f_t, \qquad \text{for } 0 \leq t < \tau$$

$$V_\tau = g$$

Note the similarity of the above expression to the heat equation from PDEs, this is not accidental, and we will examine this connection later on when we talk about the continuous analogues of these equations.

Another noteworthy remark is that one can rewrite this equation on terms of a new time variable \(q\) which represents "time left until the end of the game", i.e., \(q = \tau - t\). Under this variable, the backward Kolmogorov equation becomes

$$\Delta_q V_q = \mathcal{L} \, V_{q} + f_q, \qquad \text{for } q > 0,$$

$$V_0 = g$$

Compare this to the continuous backward heat equation with an inhomogenous term,

$$-\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial q} = \frac{1}{2} \Delta \, V + f.$$

We will examine how all of these results from the discrete case carry over into the continuous case in subsequent sections.

You should also compare it to the forward Kolmogorov equation and note that the transpose that shows up in forward equation, but not in the backward equation. A good way to make sense of this is to think of \(\mathcal{L}\) in the following way: essentially, \(\mathcal{L}^T\) generates the evolution of distributions forwards in time, while \(\mathcal{L}\) generates the evolution of value functions / test functions backwards in time. When the underlying process \(X_t\) is reversible (i.e., \(Y_t = X_{-t}\) is the same as \(X_t\), so the process looks the same when time is reversed), we have that \(\mathcal{L}^T = \mathcal{L}\) and the backwards and forwards equations are the same. This is a manifestation of the fact that the evolution of the process forward in time and backward in time are indistinguishable. This happens to be the case on grid graphs.
